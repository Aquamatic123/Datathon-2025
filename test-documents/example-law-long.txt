Digital Privacy Protection Act of 2024
United States Federal Legislation

TITLE: An Act to Protect Consumer Privacy in the Digital Age

ENACTED BY: 118th United States Congress
STATUS: Active
EFFECTIVE DATE: March 20, 2024
JURISDICTION: United States of America

SECTION 1: SHORT TITLE
This Act may be cited as the "Digital Privacy Protection Act of 2024" or "DPPA 2024".

SECTION 2: FINDINGS AND PURPOSE
(a) FINDINGS - Congress finds the following:
    (1) The digital economy has transformed how businesses collect, use, and share personal information.
    (2) Consumers have limited knowledge and control over how their personal data is collected and used.
    (3) Data breaches and unauthorized access to personal information pose significant risks to consumers.
    (4) Current federal privacy protections are outdated and fragmented across various sector-specific laws.
    (5) A comprehensive federal privacy framework is necessary to protect consumers and promote innovation.

(b) PURPOSE - The purposes of this Act are:
    (1) To establish clear, consistent privacy protections for consumers.
    (2) To give individuals meaningful control over their personal information.
    (3) To create accountability and transparency requirements for companies handling personal data.
    (4) To provide strong enforcement mechanisms and penalties for violations.
    (5) To harmonize privacy standards across the United States while allowing states to provide additional protections.

SECTION 3: DEFINITIONS
For purposes of this Act:

(1) PERSONAL INFORMATION - Any information that identifies, relates to, describes, or is capable of being associated with a particular individual, including:
    (A) Name, address, telephone number, email address
    (B) Social security number, driver's license number
    (C) Biometric information
    (D) Internet or network activity information
    (E) Geolocation data
    (F) Audio, electronic, visual, or similar information
    (G) Inferences drawn from the above to create consumer profiles

(2) COVERED ENTITY - Any person, partnership, or corporation that:
    (A) Does business in the United States
    (B) Collects, processes, or transfers personal information of 10,000 or more individuals annually
    (C) Derives more than 50% of revenue from processing or transferring personal information

(3) SENSITIVE PERSONAL INFORMATION - Personal information that reveals:
    (A) Social security number, driver's license, passport number
    (B) Financial account information, credit or debit card number
    (C) Precise geolocation
    (D) Racial or ethnic origin
    (E) Religious or philosophical beliefs
    (F) Health information
    (G) Sexual orientation or sex life
    (H) Biometric or genetic data

SECTION 4: CONSUMER RIGHTS

(a) RIGHT TO KNOW - Individuals have the right to know:
    (1) What personal information is collected about them
    (2) The sources from which information is collected
    (3) The business purposes for collecting or selling information
    (4) Third parties with whom information is shared
    (5) The retention period for personal information

(b) RIGHT TO ACCESS - Individuals have the right to request and obtain from covered entities:
    (1) A copy of their personal information in a portable format
    (2) Information about how their data is processed
    (3) Access to this information free of charge, twice per 12-month period

(c) RIGHT TO DELETION - Individuals have the right to request deletion of their personal information, subject to certain exceptions for:
    (1) Completing transactions
    (2) Detecting security incidents
    (3) Debugging to identify and repair errors
    (4) Exercising free speech
    (5) Complying with legal obligations
    (6) Internal research uses

(d) RIGHT TO CORRECTION - Individuals have the right to correct inaccurate personal information maintained by covered entities.

(e) RIGHT TO OPT-OUT - Individuals have the right to opt out of:
    (1) Sale of personal information
    (2) Sharing for cross-context behavioral advertising
    (3) Automated decision-making and profiling

(f) RIGHT TO NON-DISCRIMINATION - Covered entities may not discriminate against individuals for exercising their privacy rights, including:
    (1) Denying goods or services
    (2) Charging different prices or rates
    (3) Providing a different level of quality
    (4) Suggesting different prices or quality levels

SECTION 5: COVERED ENTITY OBLIGATIONS

(a) CONSENT REQUIREMENTS - Covered entities must obtain affirmative express consent before:
    (1) Collecting or processing sensitive personal information
    (2) Using personal information for purposes beyond original collection purpose
    (3) Transferring personal information to third parties
    (4) Using personal information for targeted advertising

(b) PRIVACY NOTICE REQUIREMENTS - Covered entities must provide clear, conspicuous privacy notices that include:
    (1) Categories of personal information collected
    (2) Purposes for collection and use
    (3) Categories of third parties with whom information is shared
    (4) Individual rights and how to exercise them
    (5) Contact information for privacy inquiries

(c) DATA MINIMIZATION - Covered entities must:
    (1) Limit collection to what is reasonably necessary and proportionate
    (2) Not collect personal information for undefined purposes
    (3) Regularly review and delete information no longer necessary

(d) DATA SECURITY - Covered entities must implement reasonable administrative, technical, and physical safeguards to protect personal information, including:
    (1) Encryption of data in transit and at rest
    (2) Access controls and authentication
    (3) Regular security assessments
    (4) Employee training on data protection
    (5) Incident response procedures

(e) PRIVACY BY DESIGN - Covered entities must implement privacy protections from the inception of products and services.

SECTION 6: ENFORCEMENT

(a) FEDERAL TRADE COMMISSION - The FTC has primary enforcement authority under this Act.

(b) STATE ATTORNEYS GENERAL - State attorneys general may bring civil actions for violations affecting state residents.

(c) CIVIL PENALTIES - Violations subject to civil penalties of:
    (1) Up to $10,000 per violation for general violations
    (2) Up to $50,000 per violation for intentional or repeated violations
    (3) Up to $100,000 per violation involving sensitive information of minors

(d) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION - Individuals may bring civil action for violations involving:
    (1) Unauthorized access or disclosure due to failure to implement reasonable security
    (2) Violations of consent requirements for sensitive information
    (3) Statutory damages: $100-$750 per violation per individual

SECTION 7: IMPACT ASSESSMENT

This legislation is expected to have significant impact on:
- Technology sector companies, particularly social media platforms, data brokers, and cloud service providers
- Marketing and advertising technology companies
- Healthcare technology and telehealth providers
- Financial services and fintech companies
- E-commerce platforms and online retailers

Market impact is estimated at 8/10, affecting companies with combined market capitalization exceeding $5 trillion. Compliance costs are projected at $10-50 million for large enterprises and $100,000-$1 million for small to medium businesses.

SECTION 8: IMPLEMENTATION TIMELINE
- Effective Date: March 20, 2024
- Large Entities (>$25M revenue): Compliance required within 180 days
- Small/Medium Entities: Compliance required within 365 days
- Regulations and guidance: FTC to issue within 90 days

SECTION 9: PREEMPTION AND STATE LAWS
This Act establishes a federal floor for privacy protections. States may enact more stringent privacy laws but may not provide less protection than established herein.

CONFIDENCE LEVEL: HIGH
This legislation represents the most comprehensive federal privacy law in U.S. history, bringing American privacy standards closer to international frameworks like GDPR while maintaining flexibility for American innovation.
